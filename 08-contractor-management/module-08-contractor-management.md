# Module 8: Contractor Management

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this module, you should be able to:

- explain why contractor management is a core FM responsibility;
- describe contractor approval, induction, RAMS review and monitoring;
- identify when a permit to work is needed;
- challenge poor contractor documentation or unsafe work;
- use contractor control language in interviews and board-level conversations.

## Phone Lesson 1: Contractor Control Starts Before Site

Contractor management starts before anyone arrives at reception.

FM should know:

- what work is being done;
- who is doing it;
- whether they are competent;
- what risks are involved;
- what site information they need;
- what evidence must be checked.

The biggest mistake is treating contractors as independent from the site. They bring expertise, but FM still controls access, information, coordination and monitoring.

## Phone Lesson 2: RAMS Must Match the Real Task

RAMS means risk assessments and method statements.

Good RAMS are specific. They explain the actual task, location, sequence, hazards, controls, competence, equipment, emergency arrangements and interfaces with the site.

Weak RAMS are generic. They mention risks that do not apply and miss the real ones.

An FM should ask:

- Does this match the work?
- Does it mention our site hazards?
- Does it consider visitors, staff and other contractors?
- Does it explain what happens if conditions change?

## Phone Lesson 3: Permits, Monitoring and Stop-Work

Some work needs a permit because normal RAMS are not enough.

Examples include hot works, roof access, electrical isolation, fire alarm isolation, confined spaces, lifting operations and intrusive works.

Monitoring does not mean standing over every contractor. It means checking controls at a frequency that matches the risk. If controls are missing, FM must be willing to stop the work.

## Practical FM Application

A contractor arrives to repair a roof leak above a public route. Their RAMS are generic and do not mention visitor segregation, fragile roof surfaces or rescue arrangements.

The correct FM response is to stop the work from starting, request revised RAMS, check competence, agree access controls, issue any required permit and communicate the visitor route impact to operations.

## Scenario Questions

### Question 1

A familiar contractor says they do not need induction because they have worked on site for years. What do you do?

### Question 2

A contractor starts using a different chemical from the one listed in their RAMS. How do you respond?

### Question 3

Hot works are complete, but the contractor wants to leave immediately. What should happen?

## Hard Questions

### Hard Question 1

Build a contractor approval process for high-risk FM contractors.

### Hard Question 2

Explain how you would manage contractor work in a live visitor attraction during peak season.

## Model Answers

### Model Answer 1

I would still require induction or a suitable refresh. Familiarity is not evidence of current site rules, changed hazards or emergency arrangements. I would keep the process proportionate but documented.

### Model Answer 2

I would pause the task and check the product, safety data sheet, COSHH assessment and whether the controls remain suitable. If the change creates new risk or has not been approved, the work should not continue until reviewed.

### Model Answer 3

Hot works need proper close-out. The permit should define fire watch and post-work checks. The contractor cannot simply leave if the permit requires monitoring, inspection or handback. FM should record completion and close the permit only when controls are satisfied.

### Model Answer 4

The approval process should check insurance, competence, training, trade qualifications, safety policy, accident history where proportionate, references, RAMS quality, safeguarding or public-facing requirements where relevant and document expiry dates. Approval should be reviewed annually and after poor performance.

### Model Answer 5

I would plan work around visitor flows, segregate work areas, schedule high-risk work out of hours where possible, brief operations, control deliveries, check emergency routes, consider animal and public interfaces, require site-specific RAMS and increase monitoring during high-footfall periods.

## Marking Guide

Strong answers include:

- pre-qualification;
- site induction;
- task-specific RAMS review;
- permit triggers;
- public and operational interface control;
- monitoring and stop-work authority;
- documented close-out.

Weak answers rely on trust, familiarity or contractor reputation instead of evidence and control.

## Interview Talking Points

Use this phrase:

> I treat contractors as part of the site's risk profile. I want competent contractors, clear scope, site-specific RAMS, induction, permit controls where needed and evidence that the work was monitored and closed properly.
