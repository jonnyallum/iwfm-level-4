## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this module, you should be able to:

- explain why Legionella control matters in FM;
- describe the purpose of a water risk assessment and written scheme;
- identify routine monitoring records and escalation triggers;
- understand the role of the Responsible Person;
- answer practical questions about flushing, temperatures, outlets and contractor reports.

## Phone Lesson 1: Why Water Hygiene Matters

Legionella bacteria can grow in water systems where conditions allow it. People can become ill when they inhale contaminated water droplets, for example from showers, taps, cooling systems or other aerosol-producing equipment.

Facilities Managers do not need to be microbiologists, but they must understand the control system:

- know the water system;
- assess the risk;
- control the conditions;
- monitor the controls;
- act when readings fail;
- keep records.

## Phone Lesson 2: Risk Assessment and Written Scheme

A Legionella risk assessment identifies water system risks and tells the organisation what control measures are needed.

The written scheme explains how those controls will be carried out. It should show:

- who is responsible;
- what assets and outlets are included;
- what checks are required;
- acceptable control limits;
- what to do if results are out of range;
- what records must be kept.

The Responsible Person must have enough authority, competence and support to make the scheme work.

## Phone Lesson 3: Monitoring and Escalation

Typical water hygiene controls include hot and cold temperature checks, flushing low-use outlets, shower cleaning, tank inspections, calorifier checks and remedial action tracking.

The key FM skill is not just collecting readings. It is noticing when control is failing.

Examples:

- cold water is too warm;
- hot water is not hot enough;
- a low-use outlet has missed flushing;
- a dead leg is identified;
- a contractor report contains repeated recommendations.

Each failure needs action, ownership and evidence.

## Practical FM Application

A monthly log shows several cold outlets above the control temperature and repeated missed flushing in a rarely used building wing.

A weak response is to file the log.

A strong response is to escalate the out-of-range results, check whether the wing is genuinely low use, increase flushing or restrict use where needed, ask the contractor or competent person for advice, track remedial work and review whether the risk assessment still reflects the building.

## Scenario Questions

### Question 1

You find three weeks of missing flushing records for low-use showers. What do you do?

### Question 2

A contractor report lists remedial works but the site team has not reviewed it. How should FM manage the report?

### Question 3

The building has changed occupancy and several areas are now rarely used. What water hygiene actions should follow?

## Hard Questions

### Hard Question 1

Explain the difference between a Legionella risk assessment, a written scheme and a water hygiene logbook.

### Hard Question 2

You are asked to reduce water hygiene contractor costs. Explain how you would review cost without weakening compliance.

## Model Answers

### Model Answer 1

I would treat the missed flushing as a control failure. I would identify which outlets were missed, arrange flushing or competent advice before normal use if needed, record the gap, investigate why it happened, update ownership or reminders and check whether any sampling or additional controls are required.

### Model Answer 2

The report should be reviewed, not just stored. I would log each recommendation, assign owners, risk-rate actions, agree timescales, keep evidence of completion and challenge unclear recommendations with the contractor. Repeated unresolved items should be escalated.

### Model Answer 3

Occupancy change can change water risk. I would review the risk assessment, update the outlet register, identify low-use outlets, adjust flushing regimes, consider removing dead legs or unused outlets and brief the Responsible Person and contractor.

### Model Answer 4

The risk assessment identifies the risks in the water system. The written scheme sets out the control measures and responsibilities. The logbook proves the checks, readings, flushing, inspections and remedial actions have been completed.

### Model Answer 5

I would review the scope against the risk assessment, asset list, task frequency, missed tasks and report quality. Savings might come from better scheduling, removing redundant outlets or clarifying scope, but I would not cut controls without competent review and documented justification.

## Marking Guide

Strong answers include:

- understanding of Legionella risk;
- clear Responsible Person ownership;
- link between risk assessment, scheme and logbook;
- action on out-of-range readings or missed checks;
- evidence and remedial tracking;
- review after occupancy or system changes.

Weak answers treat water hygiene as a contractor-only task or assume records are compliant even when failures are not acted on.

## Interview Talking Points

Use this phrase:

> I manage water hygiene by making sure the risk assessment, written scheme, routine checks and remedial actions all connect. The logbook must show control, not just activity.
