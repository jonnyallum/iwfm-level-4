## Purpose

This is the capstone assessment for the IWFM Level 4 Equivalent FM Academy. It tests whether a learner can think, decide and communicate like an operating Facilities Manager across the whole course. Sit it only after the modules, workbook and both [mock exams](../16-mock-exams/).

It is not an official IWFM qualification. It is a rigorous internal measure of readiness for an operational FM manager role and for a Longleat-style interview.

## Conditions

- **Duration:** 180 minutes, closed book.
- **Total:** 100 marks across four parts.
- **Pass:** 60%. Distinction: 80%.
- Mark with the [marking guide](../17-marking-guides/marking-guide.md).
- Answer in plain UK English, in the voice of a working FM manager.

## Part 1 — Compliance and Safety Foundations (20 marks)

Answer concisely.

**1.1 (5)** A site has working fire detection, current risk assessments and trained staff, but the latest fire door inspection is eight months overdue. Is the site compliant? Explain your reasoning and your action.

**1.2 (5)** A water temperature monitoring regime exists but a closed wing is about to reopen to visitors after six months. What must happen before the outlets are used, and why?

**1.3 (5)** A near-miss is reported where a visitor was nearly struck by a falling object near contractor works. No one was hurt. Explain why this still matters and what you do.

**1.4 (5)** Explain the hierarchy of control and why administrative controls and PPE sit near the bottom.

## Part 2 — Money and Assets (20 marks)

**2.1 (10)** An ageing air handling unit serving the main restaurant has been repaired three times this year from the reactive budget. Build the business case to replace it from capital, including how you would present it to a board that only asks for the cheapest option.

**2.2 (10)** You inherit an estate with a growing maintenance backlog, three plant items over fifteen years old, and no reliable asset register. Set out a one-year plan to bring the estate under control, in priority order, with the reasoning for each step.

## Part 3 — Leadership and Strategy (20 marks)

**3.1 (10)** The board sees FM as an overhead and wants to cut its budget. Write how you would reframe FM as a business enabler and protect the spend that must not be cut. Use the language a board acts on.

**3.2 (10)** Describe how you would move an estate and its team from reactive firefighting to planned, strategic FM over twelve to eighteen months, covering compliance, data, culture and reporting.

## Part 4 — Integrated Scenario (40 marks)

> This part carries the most marks and tests whether you can hold safety, operations, money and people together under pressure.

**4.1** You are the FM manager at a large visitor attraction in peak season. Over one weekend the following unfolds:

- **Saturday morning:** a chiller serving the main restaurant fails; temporary cooling is available but limited.
- **Saturday midday:** a contractor doing roof work has generic RAMS and wants to start above a public route; separately, the fire alarm in that building needs isolating for the work.
- **Saturday afternoon:** heavy rain floods part of the car park and a tree near a footpath is reported as unstable.
- **Sunday morning:** the board chair messages you directly, worried about visitor experience and asking why a restaurant area was warm yesterday and whether the site is safe to keep trading.

Write a full account of how you manage the weekend. Your answer should cover:

- how you triage and prioritise across all the events;
- the specific control actions for each (chiller, RAMS/permit/alarm isolation, flooding, tree, restaurant);
- how you protect life safety and statutory compliance without closing the attraction unnecessarily;
- how you communicate with operations, contractors, your team and the board chair, in the right language for each;
- what records and follow-up you put in place;
- and the underlying principles that drove your decisions.

Justify your priority order throughout.

## Marking

Use the [marking guide](../17-marking-guides/marking-guide.md). Apply the four strands (knowledge, application, judgement, communication) and the automatic caps. Part 4 is where Distinction is won or lost: the strongest answers prioritise life safety and compliance instinctively, keep trading through contingency rather than knee-jerk closure, control the contractor and alarm isolation by permit with cover, manage the estate-wide weather events as a connected system, and handle the board chair with honest, business-framed communication and a clear plan — never a bluff.

### Distinction indicators

- Decisions are made and defended, not hedged.
- Safety and statutory duties are never traded for trading.
- Whole-life cost, risk-based prioritisation and no-surprises communication appear naturally.
- The board chair is answered honestly, in business language, with a plan attached.
- Records, review dates and follow-up are built in throughout.
