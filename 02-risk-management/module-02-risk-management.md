# Module 2: Risk Management

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this module, you should be able to:

- explain risk in FM terms;
- distinguish hazard, risk, likelihood and consequence;
- apply risk-based prioritisation;
- create a clear evidence trail;
- explain why risk assessment is a management process, not a form.

## Phone Lesson 1: Hazard vs Risk

A hazard is something with the potential to cause harm.

A risk is the chance that harm will happen and how serious it could be.

Example:

- Hazard: damaged stair nosing.
- Risk: a person trips, falls and is injured.

Facilities Managers must not only spot hazards. They must judge risk and decide proportionate controls.

## Phone Lesson 2: Likelihood and Consequence

Risk is normally judged through likelihood and consequence.

A low-likelihood risk can still be urgent if the consequence is severe. A high-likelihood issue can also become urgent if it happens often enough to show a failing control.

Example:

A blocked fire exit may not cause harm today, but if there is a fire, the consequence could be fatal. That makes it a high-priority issue.

## Phone Lesson 3: The Hierarchy of Control

The best control removes the hazard. The weakest control relies only on people behaving perfectly.

A practical hierarchy is:

1. Eliminate the hazard.
2. Substitute with something safer.
3. Use engineering controls.
4. Use administrative controls.
5. Use PPE as the last line of defence.

In FM, this might mean repairing a defect rather than just putting a sign beside it.

## Practical FM Application

A wet floor sign is not a complete control if the leak continues for days. It may be a temporary warning, but the real control is to stop the leak, restrict access, dry the area and prevent recurrence.

## Scenario Questions

### Question 1

A fire door does not close fully because the floor has swollen. Staff have been pulling it shut manually. How do you assess and control the risk?

### Question 2

A plant room is cluttered with contractor materials after several small jobs. There is no immediate incident, but access to valves is restricted. What is your response?

### Question 3

A footpath used by visitors has uneven paving. There has been one near miss but no injury. How do you prioritise it?

## Hard Questions

### Hard Question 1

You have 40 open defects and budget to fix only 10 this month. Create a prioritisation method that you could defend to a director, auditor or enforcing authority.

### Hard Question 2

A department manager keeps asking you to delay disruptive statutory maintenance because it affects their service. Explain how you would manage the conflict.

## Model Answers

### Model Answer 1

The fire door is a life-safety asset, so it should be treated as high priority. I would inspect and record the defect, check whether it affects compartmentation or an escape route, raise an urgent corrective action and consider temporary compensatory measures until repaired. Staff manually closing it is not a reliable control. I would record the issue in the fire action tracker and review whether other fire doors have similar problems.

### Model Answer 2

Restricted access in a plant room is a risk because emergency isolation, maintenance and inspection may be delayed. I would stop further storage, require the contractor or site team to clear the area, check whether any escape or isolation routes are compromised, photograph and log the issue, and reinforce contractor housekeeping requirements. If repeated, it becomes a contractor performance issue.

### Model Answer 3

I would assess footfall, user vulnerability, lighting, weather exposure, severity of unevenness, previous near misses and whether alternative routes exist. If the route is public-facing and high-footfall, it should be prioritised before an injury occurs. Temporary controls may include barriers, warning signage or route diversion, but a permanent repair should be logged and tracked.

## Marking Guide

Strong answers include:

- hazard identification;
- likelihood and consequence;
- immediate controls;
- permanent controls;
- evidence;
- ownership;
- escalation;
- review of recurrence.

Weak answers treat risk assessment as a tick-box form.

## Interview Talking Points

Use this phrase:

> My risk approach is practical. I make the situation safe first, then assess likelihood and consequence, assign ownership, record evidence and make sure the permanent control is completed.
