# Module 4: Fire Safety

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this module, you should be able to:

- explain the FM role in day-to-day fire safety;
- identify fire safety evidence that must be maintained;
- understand how fire risk assessment actions become FM tasks;
- spot common fire safety red flags in buildings;
- answer interview questions about fire doors, alarms, emergency lighting and evacuation controls.

## Phone Lesson 1: Fire Safety Is a Management System

Fire safety is not only alarms and extinguishers. It is a system made of risk assessment, maintenance, inspection, housekeeping, training, evacuation planning and corrective action.

The Responsible Person must make sure fire risks are assessed and controlled. FM often supports this by managing the building systems and evidence.

Core FM fire controls include:

- fire alarm testing and servicing;
- emergency lighting checks;
- fire door inspections;
- escape route checks;
- extinguisher servicing;
- contractor hot works control;
- fire risk assessment action tracking.

## Phone Lesson 2: Fire Doors and Escape Routes

Fire doors protect escape routes and slow the spread of smoke and fire. A fire door that does not close is not a minor cosmetic issue.

Escape routes must be available, clear and usable. That means no storage, no wedged doors, no locked exits without proper release arrangements and no unmanaged changes to layouts.

In FM, small habits matter. A blocked corridor or wedged fire door may look normal because people walk past it every day. The FM job is to treat those signs as control failures, not background noise.

## Phone Lesson 3: Testing, Records and Action Tracking

Fire safety records need to show that checks are happening and that failures are acted on.

Typical records include:

- fire alarm weekly tests;
- fire alarm servicing;
- emergency lighting monthly functional tests and annual duration tests;
- fire door inspections;
- extinguisher servicing;
- evacuation drills;
- fire risk assessment actions;
- hot works permits;
- staff training records.

Testing without action is weak compliance. If a fault is found, the record must show what was done, who owns it and when it was closed.

## Practical FM Application

During an inspection, you find a final exit obstructed by deliveries, a fire door wedged open and two overdue fire risk assessment actions.

A strong FM response is immediate: clear the exit, remove the wedge, record the issue, speak to the local manager, check whether this is repeated behaviour, update the fire action tracker and escalate overdue actions by risk.

## Scenario Questions

### Question 1

A tenant says they need to wedge a fire door open because the area is too hot and busy. How do you respond?

### Question 2

The weekly fire alarm test has been missed for three weeks because the usual site colleague was on leave. What should you do?

### Question 3

A fire risk assessment action requires replacement fire stopping above a ceiling, but the work is expensive and disruptive. How do you manage it?

## Hard Questions

### Hard Question 1

Explain the difference between a fire risk assessment and a fire safety maintenance regime, and how FM should connect the two.

### Hard Question 2

You inherit a site with poor fire records, no clear fire action tracker and several unknown door defects. Set out your first 30 days of action.

## Model Answers

### Model Answer 1

I would not accept wedging the fire door as a normal control. I would explain the purpose of the door, remove the wedge and look for a proper solution such as ventilation, an approved hold-open device linked to the alarm or changes to local arrangements. I would record the issue and monitor recurrence.

### Model Answer 2

I would restart the weekly testing immediately, record the missed checks honestly and put resilience in place so the task does not depend on one person. That might include a deputy, calendar reminders, a compliance dashboard and management review of missed statutory or life-safety checks.

### Model Answer 3

I would treat the action according to the fire risk, not only the cost. I would understand the risk assessor's finding, seek competent advice if needed, apply temporary controls where appropriate, get quotes, assign ownership and escalate funding decisions with a clear record of risk and timescale.

### Model Answer 4

The fire risk assessment identifies hazards, people at risk and required improvements. The maintenance regime keeps fire safety systems and precautions working day to day. FM connects them by turning assessment findings into tracked actions and maintaining evidence that alarms, lighting, doors, extinguishers and escape routes are checked.

### Model Answer 5

In the first 30 days, I would locate the latest fire risk assessment, create a fire action tracker, identify overdue high-risk actions, inspect escape routes and critical doors, confirm alarm and emergency lighting service status, rebuild missing logbook evidence where possible, assign owners and brief senior management on immediate risks and priorities.

## Marking Guide

Strong answers include:

- recognition of life-safety priority;
- immediate controls for blocked routes or failed doors;
- link between risk assessment and action tracking;
- clear evidence expectations;
- escalation of overdue high-risk actions;
- practical alternatives rather than unsafe compromise.

Weak answers rely on informal fixes, ignore repeated behaviours or treat fire records as an admin task only.

## Interview Talking Points

Use this phrase:

> I see fire safety as a live management system. The fire risk assessment tells us what needs controlling, and the FM regime proves that alarms, lighting, doors, routes and actions are being managed.
