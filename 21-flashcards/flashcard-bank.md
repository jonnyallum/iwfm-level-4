## Purpose

Phone-first recall cards covering the whole course. Each card is a **prompt** and a **flip** (the answer). Cover the flip, say your answer out loud, then check. These are for fast, repeated recall — the spaced-repetition layer that makes module knowledge stick.

Tags map to modules so weak areas route back to the right lesson. For applied practice use the [workbook](../14-workbook/README.md); for spoken practice use the [crash plan](../19-interview-prep/7-day-longleat-crash-plan.md).

> How to drill: one module's cards per session, out loud, twice through. Mark any you miss and re-drill those tomorrow.

## Module 1 — FM Principles `#fm-principles`

**Q: In one line, what is the purpose of FM?**
> Keep the built environment safe, compliant, efficient and fit for the organisation's core purpose, so the business can operate.

**Q: Hard FM vs soft FM?**
> Hard: mechanical/electrical, fabric, fire systems, lifts. Soft: cleaning, security, catering support, grounds, waste, helpdesk.

**Q: Why is FM a business function, not an overhead?**
> A failed asset or unsafe area closes revenue space, triggers claims and damages reputation. FM protects continuity, brand and income.

## Module 2 — Risk Management `#risk-management`

**Q: Hazard vs risk?**
> Hazard = potential to cause harm. Risk = likelihood × consequence of that harm occurring.

**Q: Five steps of risk assessment?**
> Identify hazards; decide who's harmed and how; evaluate and decide controls; record findings; review and update.

**Q: Hierarchy of control, top to bottom?**
> Eliminate, substitute, engineering controls, administrative controls, PPE.

**Q: Why is "be careful / PPE" near the bottom?**
> It relies on people behaving perfectly every time, so it fails more easily than designing the hazard out.

## Module 3 — Health & Safety `#health-safety`

**Q: What does HSWA 1974 require of an employer (broadly)?**
> Ensure, so far as is reasonably practicable, the health, safety and welfare of employees and others affected — safe systems, training, information, supervision, safe environment.

**Q: What is a near-miss and why does it matter?**
> An event that nearly caused harm. It is a free warning — record, investigate and fix the control before it becomes an injury.

**Q: What is RIDDOR?**
> Reporting of Injuries, Diseases and Dangerous Occurrences Regulations — duty to report specified injuries, diseases and dangerous occurrences.

## Module 4 — Fire Safety `#fire-safety`

**Q: Who is the "responsible person" under the Fire Safety Order 2005?**
> The person with control of the premises (often employer/occupier), who must do and maintain the fire risk assessment and precautions.

**Q: Why is a wedged-open fire door dangerous?**
> It defeats compartmentation and lets fire and smoke spread along the escape route.

**Q: How do you control a fire-alarm isolation for contractor work?**
> Permit-controlled: minimum scope/time, fire-watch cover, areas informed, isolation logged, confirmed reinstatement and test before sign-off.

## Module 5 — Water Hygiene `#water-hygiene`

**Q: What is Legionella and why does it matter?**
> A bacterium causing Legionnaires' disease (a potentially fatal pneumonia) when contaminated water droplets are inhaled.

**Q: Conditions that let Legionella grow?**
> Stagnant water, ~20-45°C, nutrients/biofilm/scale, and droplet generation.

**Q: Two temperature principles that control it?**
> Keep hot water hot and cold water cold; keep water moving (flush low-use outlets).

**Q: What does ACOP L8 require?**
> Identify and assess risk, appoint a competent person, control via a written scheme, monitor and record, and review.

## Module 6 — Building Systems `#building-systems`

**Q: What does a BMS give an FM?**
> Central monitoring and control of building services: visibility, alarms, scheduling, energy data and diagnostics.

**Q: Why diagnose before condemning plant?**
> Comfort complaints are often zoning, controls or balancing issues, not failures. Check the data and walk the area first.

**Q: How far do you trust a BMS dashboard?**
> Trust but verify — it reflects its sensors, which can be wrong or miscalibrated. Believe credible human reports and check on site.

## Module 7 — Asset Management `#asset-management`

**Q: What does an asset register hold?**
> Identity/location, type, age, condition, expected and remaining life, maintenance history, criticality and replacement cost.

**Q: Reactive vs planned vs predictive maintenance?**
> Reactive: fix on failure. Planned: scheduled prevention. Predictive: condition/data-based intervention before failure.

**Q: Why is a growing maintenance backlog a warning?**
> It means the estate is drifting toward failures and a future cost spike — invest before something breaks in peak season.

---

## Module 8 — Contractor Management `#contractor-management`

**Q: What are RAMS and what makes them good?**
> Risk assessments and method statements. Good ones are task- and site-specific; generic ones list irrelevant risks and miss real ones.

**Q: When is a permit to work needed? Examples?**
> When normal RAMS aren't enough: hot works, height/roof, electrical isolation, fire-alarm isolation, confined space, lifting.

**Q: A trusted contractor says they don't need induction. Response?**
> Familiarity isn't evidence of current rules or hazards. Require induction or a documented refresh — politely but firmly.

## Module 9 — Budgeting & Finance `#budgeting-finance`

**Q: Opex vs capex?**
> Opex = recurring running cost (repairs, contracts, utilities). Capex = one-off investment in a lasting asset (boiler, roof, chiller).

**Q: What is whole-life cost?**
> Total cost over an asset's life: purchase, energy, maintenance, downtime, failure risk and disposal — not just the purchase price.

**Q: What is the "no surprises" rule?**
> Flag budget pressure early, with a recovery plan. Bad news early is recoverable; a month-eleven surprise is not.

**Q: Asked to cut 10% — what do you protect?**
> Statutory and safety-critical lines (fire, water, gas, electrical, lifting, asbestos). Save on discretionary work instead.

## Module 10 — Leadership & Communication `#leadership-communication`

**Q: Three consistent FM leadership behaviours?**
> Be clear what good looks like; be visible when things go wrong; make the call, own it, explain it.

**Q: Why tell the same fact three ways?**
> Board hears money/risk/reputation/continuity; peers hear impact/coordination; workshop hears task/safety/respect.

**Q: Board challenges a figure you don't have. What do you say?**
> Don't bluff. Give the honest position and commit to the exact number by a stated time — then deliver it.

---

## Module 11 — Strategic FM `#strategic-fm`

**Q: Operational vs tactical vs strategic FM?**
> Operational = today (the call-out). Tactical = this year (programme, budget, contracts). Strategic = 3-5 years (where the estate is going).

**Q: Name four FM KPIs.**
> Statutory compliance %; planned-vs-reactive ratio; asset condition/remaining life; SLA response times (also energy trend, backlog).

**Q: How do you make the investment case to a board?**
> In their language: visitor experience, reputation, revenue continuity, cost — with evidence and the cost of doing nothing.

---

## Module 12 — Estate Management `#estate-management`

**Q: What does estate management cover beyond buildings?**
> Land, grounds, roads, car parks, paths, drainage, boundaries, signage, utilities infrastructure and multiple buildings as one system.

**Q: Four underrated external-estate risks?**
> Trees (survey regime), paths/roads (inspection), drainage/flood (culvert checks), boundaries (containment + recorded responsibility).

**Q: How does heritage/listed status change safety work?**
> It governs the method, not the outcome. Least-intrusive solution, specialists, consent — but safety still wins if they conflict.

## Cross-Cutting Principles `#principles`

**Q: The five things to walk into an interview believing?**
> Safety is never traded; decisions not hedging; evidence is the defence; whole-life cost beats lowest price; no surprises.

**Q: First move with almost any incident?**
> Make it safe first (cordon/restrict/secure), then solve the underlying problem and record it.

**Q: What is the record for?**
> It is the evidence and the defence — for you, the team and the organisation — if something fails or an auditor asks.
