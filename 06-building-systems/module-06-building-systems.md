## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this module, you should be able to:

- explain the main building systems an FM team manages;
- understand why building systems are linked to compliance, safety and business continuity;
- distinguish planned maintenance from reactive repair;
- identify evidence needed for critical systems;
- discuss building systems confidently in an FM interview.

## Phone Lesson 1: What Building Systems Mean in FM

Building systems are the services that make a site safe, usable and compliant.

Typical systems include:

- electrical distribution;
- heating, ventilation and air conditioning;
- water systems;
- fire alarm and emergency lighting;
- lifts and lifting equipment;
- access control and security;
- drainage;
- building fabric, roofs and doors;
- generators and UPS where present.

An FM does not need to personally repair every system. The FM role is to know what exists, how critical it is, who maintains it, what evidence proves it is safe and what happens when it fails.

## Phone Lesson 2: Criticality and Failure Impact

Not all building systems have the same risk.

A failed decorative light is inconvenient. A failed fire alarm panel, lift emergency communication, cold water control, electrical distribution board or animal enclosure gate can become a safety, compliance or operational incident.

A practical FM question is:

> If this system fails today, who is affected and how quickly does it matter?

This helps prioritise maintenance, spares, contractor call-outs and escalation.

## Phone Lesson 3: Maintenance, Evidence and Handover

Good building systems management depends on records.

FM needs:

- asset registers;
- service schedules;
- contractor reports;
- statutory inspection records;
- defect logs;
- drawings and O&M manuals;
- emergency contacts;
- lifecycle condition notes.

The hardest problems often happen at handover. A new FM inherits missing drawings, unknown assets, vague service contracts and open defects. The answer is to rebuild control: list critical systems, find evidence gaps, assign owners and prioritise high-risk items.

## Practical FM Application

A site has repeated heating complaints, an overdue EICR remedial action and an unreliable lift. Treating all three as equal would be poor FM.

The EICR action may be a compliance and safety priority. The lift may affect accessibility and visitor movement. Heating may be urgent in vulnerable-user areas but less urgent in a store room. The FM decision should be based on risk, impact, evidence and user need.

## Scenario Questions

### Question 1

A fire alarm panel shows an intermittent fault during opening hours. What do you do?

### Question 2

You discover a plant room contains equipment that is not on the asset register. How do you regain control?

### Question 3

A lift contractor reports recurring faults but no permanent recommendation. How do you challenge this?

## Hard Questions

### Hard Question 1

Create a method for ranking building systems by criticality across a visitor attraction.

### Hard Question 2

Explain how you would prepare building systems information for a new FM joining the site.

## Model Answers

### Model Answer 1

I would treat the fire alarm panel fault as a life-safety issue. I would contact the fire alarm contractor, check whether the system remains operational, record the fault, consider temporary measures, inform the Responsible Person or duty manager and track the repair to completion.

### Model Answer 2

I would identify the equipment, photograph it, check labels and drawings, ask maintenance contractors for history, assess criticality, add it to the asset register and decide whether it needs inspection, servicing or statutory compliance evidence.

### Model Answer 3

I would ask for root cause analysis, fault history, parts replaced, recommended permanent action and risk of failure. If the contractor cannot give a clear answer, I would escalate through the contract manager or seek a second competent view.

### Model Answer 4

I would score systems by life safety, legal duty, public impact, animal welfare impact, business interruption, repair lead time, redundancy and failure history. Fire, electrical, water, lifts, enclosure infrastructure and emergency power would likely rank highly.

### Model Answer 5

I would prepare an asset register, statutory compliance matrix, contractor list, open defects, critical spares list, drawings, emergency contacts, service contracts and current risks. The aim is to make the new FM operationally effective quickly.

## Marking Guide

Strong answers include:

- criticality thinking;
- life-safety prioritisation;
- clear evidence expectations;
- contractor challenge;
- asset register discipline;
- escalation where systems affect visitors, staff or compliance.

Weak answers focus only on fixing complaints and ignore statutory evidence or system failure impact.

## Interview Talking Points

Use this phrase:

> I manage building systems by understanding criticality, keeping asset and compliance records current, using competent contractors and making sure defects turn into tracked actions rather than disappearing inside service reports.
