# Module 1: Facilities Management Principles

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this module, you should be able to:

- explain what Facilities Management actually does;
- distinguish reactive maintenance from managed FM;
- describe the relationship between people, place and process;
- apply safety, compliance, operations and finance thinking to decisions;
- answer interview questions at operational manager level.

## Phone Lesson 1: What FM Actually Is

Facilities Management is the discipline of keeping buildings, assets, services and workplaces safe, compliant, usable and effective.

A maintenance person may fix a broken door. A Facilities Manager asks why the door failed, whether it creates a fire or security risk, who is affected, whether inspections missed it, what evidence is needed and how to prevent recurrence.

FM sits between the physical site and the organisation's ability to function.

## Phone Lesson 2: The Four Pillars

Every FM decision sits across four pillars.

### Safety

Could someone be harmed?

### Compliance

Could the organisation breach a legal or regulatory duty?

### Operations

Could the site, service or business be disrupted?

### Finance

What is the cost of action compared with the cost of failure?

A strong FM does not chase the loudest complaint. They prioritise based on risk, legal duty and operational consequence.

## Phone Lesson 3: People, Place and Process

FM is often described as managing people, place and process.

### People

Employees, visitors, contractors, residents, tenants, volunteers and regulators.

### Place

Buildings, grounds, roads, plant rooms, toilets, offices, kitchens, stores, car parks and public routes.

### Process

Inspections, permits, maintenance, risk assessments, cleaning, audits, emergency plans and evidence trails.

When these three are aligned, a site runs smoothly. When one fails, the others usually suffer.

## Practical FM Application

Imagine a blocked visitor toilet in a high-footfall attraction.

A weak response is:

> Call someone to unblock it.

A manager-level FM response is:

1. Assess whether the area is safe and hygienic.
2. Close the facility if needed.
3. Signpost alternative toilets.
4. Log the defect.
5. Assign contractor or internal maintenance.
6. Check whether this is recurring.
7. Review cleaning and inspection frequency.
8. Record closure and reopening evidence.

That is FM thinking.

## Scenario Questions

### Question 1

A staff member reports a leaking ceiling tile above an office desk. The leak is small but near electrical equipment. What do you do first, and what do you do next?

### Question 2

A visitor complains that a corridor is too dark. Maintenance says the lights still work, but they are dim. How do you decide whether this is a priority?

### Question 3

A contractor says they can start urgent roof repairs immediately, but they have not submitted RAMS yet. The roof leak is causing disruption. What is your decision?

## Hard Questions

### Hard Question 1

You inherit a site where almost all FM work is reactive. There is no reliable asset register, contractors are called only when something breaks and inspection records are inconsistent. Create a 90-day stabilisation plan.

### Hard Question 2

A director wants cosmetic improvements before an open day. At the same time, you have overdue emergency lighting actions. Budget only allows one set of works this month. Explain your recommendation and evidence trail.

## Model Answers

### Model Answer 1

The first action is to make the area safe. Move people away from the leak, isolate or protect electrical equipment if safe to do so, and escalate if there is any electrical risk. Then log the defect, identify the source, assess whether the ceiling or electrics are compromised, assign a contractor or maintenance lead and record all actions. The risk assessment should consider safety, business disruption and recurrence.

### Model Answer 2

I would assess the corridor use, footfall, vulnerable users, trip hazards, CCTV/security implications and whether lighting levels affect safe movement. If the corridor is part of an escape route, fire safety priority increases. I would record the complaint, inspect the area, log any lux-level or visual findings if available, prioritise based on risk and create a corrective action.

### Model Answer 3

The contractor should not start high-risk roof work without suitable RAMS. I would control the leak temporarily if possible, isolate affected areas and request urgent RAMS review. If the situation creates immediate danger, emergency controls are still required, but lack of RAMS is not ignored. The decision and temporary controls must be recorded.

## Marking Guide

Strong answers include:

- immediate safety control;
- risk-based prioritisation;
- legal or compliance awareness;
- clear ownership;
- evidence capture;
- communication;
- prevention of recurrence.

Weak answers only describe fixing the visible defect.

## Interview Talking Points

Use this phrase:

> My approach to FM is risk-led. I look at safety, compliance, operations and cost, then build systems that prevent repeat failures rather than just reacting to defects.
