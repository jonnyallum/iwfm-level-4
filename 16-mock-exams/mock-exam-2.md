> Closed book. 90 minutes. Answer all three sections in your own words. Mark with the [marking guide](../17-marking-guides/marking-guide.md). Total 100 marks.

## Section A — Knowledge (30 marks, 5 each)

**A1.** Define hazard and risk and explain the difference.

**A2.** What does the Health and Safety at Work etc. Act 1974 require of an employer in broad terms?

**A3.** What is RIDDOR, and give one reportable example on a visitor attraction.

**A4.** What is whole-life cost and why is it preferred to lowest price?

**A5.** Explain operational, tactical and strategic FM with a time horizon for each.

**A6.** Name four external-estate risks and the regime that controls each.

## Section B — Application (30 marks, 10 each)

**B1.** Finance asks you to cut 10% from the FM budget. Explain where you look first, what you refuse to cut, and how you present the trade-offs.

**B2.** A technician keeps skipping planned-maintenance sign-off, saying the paperwork is pointless. Describe how you handle the conversation and why the record matters.

**B3.** The board wants to grow visitor numbers by 20%. Explain what that means for FM and the estate, and how you would prepare.

## Section C — Scenario (40 marks)

**C1.** After heavy overnight rain, you arrive to open a visitor attraction and find:

- the main car park is flooded and unusable;
- a section of access road surface has broken up;
- a large tree near a public footpath has dropped a limb;
- a listed building on the route has a section of loose masonry overhead.

Explain how you manage this as an estate-wide event: your immediate safety actions, how you keep the site open or decide not to, how you investigate root causes, who you communicate with, and what you put in place so it is foreseen next time. Justify your priority order, and explain how the listed status changes your approach to the masonry.

## Marking Notes

- Section C carries the most marks. A pass must make life-safety hazards safe first (tree limb, loose masonry), redirect visitors/traffic, investigate drainage as the root cause, and set up proportionate inspection regimes. Heritage must be treated as a constraint on method, not a reason to delay making people safe.
- Apply the automatic caps: any answer that lets conservation rules stall an immediate safety action, or reopens areas with no evidence of control, is capped at Borderline.
- Award across knowledge, application, judgement and communication strands.

## Model Answer Pointers

- **A1-A6:** see the corresponding workbook files for model points (Modules 2, 3, 9, 11, 12).
- **B1:** protect statutory/safety-critical lines; save on discretionary/decoration/re-tender; model the risk behind each cut so the board chooses knowingly.
- **B2:** acknowledge skill and completed work; the record is the evidence that protects him/the team in failure or audit; agree the standard, set a review date, follow up.
- **B3:** more load on every system (wear, utilities, toilets, catering, parking, emergency capacity); assess what fails first; build investment and resilience to support growth safely.
- **C1:** Make the tree limb and loose masonry safe and cordon immediately (life safety first). Redirect visitors/traffic from the flooded car park and broken road; decide on partial opening only where safe. Investigate drainage/culverts as the flood root cause; arrange tree, surface and masonry works. For the listed masonry: make safe now, then engage conservation specialists and secure consent for the repair method — safety first, heritage governs the method. Communicate with operations, management and specialists; keep a decision log. Put in place drainage, tree and fabric inspection regimes so the combination is foreseen.
