## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this module, you should be able to:

- lead a mixed team of in-house staff and contractors;
- communicate up to a board and down to a workshop in the right language;
- handle conflict, underperformance and pushback without losing the room;
- run a clear handover, brief and toolbox talk;
- stay calm and decisive in an incident.

## Phone Lesson 1: Lead the Work, Not Just the People

FM leadership is operational. You are judged on whether the estate runs safely and the team trusts your decisions under pressure.

Good FM leaders do three things consistently:

- they are clear about what good looks like, so no one guesses the standard;
- they are visible, especially when things go wrong, not only when they go well;
- they make the call, take ownership of it, and explain it afterwards.

You do not need to be the most technical person in the room. You need to set the standard, remove blockers, back your team in public, and correct them in private.

## Phone Lesson 2: Speak Up and Down in the Right Language

The same fact has to be told three ways.

To a board: money, risk, reputation and continuity. "If we do not replace this chiller, we risk closing the restaurant in peak season and losing a day's revenue."

To a peer or operations manager: impact and coordination. "I need this area for two hours on Tuesday; here is how we keep visitors clear."

To the workshop: task, safety and respect. "Here is the job, here is the risk, here is what I need from you, what am I missing?"

The skill is translation. A weak FM uses maintenance jargon with the board and corporate language with the workshop. A strong FM reads the audience and changes register without changing the facts.

## Phone Lesson 3: Conflict, Underperformance and the Hard Conversation

Avoiding a hard conversation is a decision, and usually the wrong one.

Handle underperformance early, factually and privately:

- describe the specific behaviour or outcome, not the personality;
- explain the impact on safety, the team or the work;
- agree a clear, supported expectation and a date to review;
- record it proportionately.

Handle pushback by listening first. Often resistance carries real information about a risk you have not seen. If they are right, change the plan and say so. If they are wrong, explain the decision and hold it. Either way the team learns that you are fair and that the standard is real.

## Practical FM Application

A long-serving technician keeps skipping the sign-off on planned maintenance tasks, saying the paperwork is pointless because the work is always done.

The correct FM response is a direct, private conversation: acknowledge the skill and the completed work, but be clear that the record is the evidence that protects him, the team and the organisation if something fails or an auditor asks. Explain the consequence of no evidence, agree the standard, set a review date, and follow up so the standard is seen to be real.

## Scenario Questions

### Question 1

Two members of your team have stopped speaking and it is affecting shift handovers. What do you do?

### Question 2

The board challenges your spend in a meeting and you do not have the figure to hand. How do you respond in the room?

### Question 3

A contractor is rude to a junior member of your team in front of visitors. What is your immediate response?

## Hard Questions

### Hard Question 1

How do you build trust with an in-house team that has seen managers come and go?

### Hard Question 2

How do you communicate during a live incident so the right things happen and the right people are informed?

## Model Answers

### Model Answer 1

I would speak to each person separately and factually, focusing on the operational impact on handover and safety rather than taking sides. I would set the clear expectation that handovers happen properly regardless of personal differences, look for a practical fix such as a structured handover sheet, and review it. If it continued, I would treat it as a performance and conduct issue, not just a personality clash.

### Model Answer 2

I would not bluff. I would acknowledge the challenge, give the honest position, and commit to a precise figure by a stated time: "I do not have the exact number in front of me; I will send it by end of day with the breakdown." Then I would deliver it. Credibility comes from being reliable, not from pretending to know.

### Model Answer 3

I would step in calmly and immediately, move the conversation away from visitors, and make clear that behaviour is not acceptable on site. I would support my team member in the moment and deal with the contractor afterwards, including a record and, if needed, escalation to their employer. Protecting my team and the visitor experience comes first; the detail is handled away from the public.

### Model Answer 4

Trust is earned through consistency. I would be visible early, listen before changing anything, deliver small promises reliably, back the team in public, and be honest when I do not know something. I would not over-promise or rubbish the previous regime. Doing what I said I would do, repeatedly, is what turns a sceptical team around.

### Model Answer 5

In an incident I keep communication short, factual and role-based. I confirm who is doing what, what the immediate priority is (life safety first), and who needs to be informed: emergency services, senior management, affected operations and, where relevant, visitors. I avoid speculation, keep a simple log of decisions and times, and give one clear point of contact so information does not fragment.

## Marking Guide

Strong answers include:

- audience-appropriate communication up and down;
- early, factual handling of conflict and underperformance;
- visibility and ownership under pressure;
- honesty over bluffing;
- protecting the team and the visitor experience;
- clear, role-based incident communication.

Weak answers avoid hard conversations, use one register for every audience, bluff under challenge, or lead only through authority rather than trust.

## Interview Talking Points

Use this phrase:

> I lead by setting a clear standard, being visible when things go wrong, and translating the same facts for the board, for operations and for the workshop. I deal with problems early and honestly, and I back my team in public.
