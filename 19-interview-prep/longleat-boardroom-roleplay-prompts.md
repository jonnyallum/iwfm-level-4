## Purpose

Live role-play prompts for rehearsing spoken FM answers under pressure. A second person — or an AI tutor — reads the **interviewer lines** out loud. You answer as the FM. The reader then uses the **press-harder follow-ups** to test whether you hold your judgement, and the **what good sounds like** notes to give feedback.

These build on the [Longleat Boardroom Simulation](../13-longleat-simulation/longleat-boardroom-simulation.md) and feed the [7-day crash plan](7-day-longleat-crash-plan.md). Mark spoken answers with the [spoken-answer rubric](../17-marking-guides/spoken-answer-rubric.md).

## How to Run a Session

1. Reader picks a scenario and reads the opening line in character.
2. Candidate answers out loud, in STAR shape, making a clear decision.
3. Reader uses two or three press-harder follow-ups to apply pressure.
4. Reader scores against the [spoken-answer rubric](../17-marking-guides/spoken-answer-rubric.md) and gives one strength and one improvement.
5. Swap and repeat.

> Reader's job is to be a realistic board, not a hostile one. Pressure tests judgement; it does not try to trick. The candidate should never be rewarded for caving on safety.

## Scenario 1 — The Cost-Cutting Board

**Interviewer (board chair):** "Facilities is one of our biggest controllable costs. I want a 15% cut next year. Tell me where it comes from."

**Press-harder follow-ups:**
- "Why can't you just defer some of that statutory testing for a year? Nothing's gone wrong before."
- "Our competitor spends less than us. Are you over-maintaining?"
- "Give me one number now — how much can you save by Friday?"

**What good sounds like:**
- Protects statutory and safety-critical spend explicitly and refuses to cut it.
- Offers real savings from discretionary work, re-tendering and waste.
- Models the risk behind any cut so the board chooses knowingly.
- Does not bluff a figure; commits to a precise, evidenced number by a stated time.

**Red flags:** agrees to defer compliance to hit the target; invents a saving figure on the spot.

## Scenario 2 — Pressure to Keep Trading

**Interviewer (operations director):** "You want to close the main restaurant in peak season over a chiller? Do you know what that costs us a day? Keep it open."

**Press-harder follow-ups:**
- "It's only food getting a bit warm. Run it and we'll fix it overnight."
- "If you close it, that's on you. The board will want to know why."
- "What if I tell you to keep it open anyway?"

**What good sounds like:**
- Separates the genuine safety/food-hygiene line from the commercial pressure.
- Looks for a way to keep trading safely first (temporary cooling, contingency) rather than a binary close/stay-open.
- Will close or restrict if food safety cannot be assured, and explains the liability and reputation cost of getting it wrong.
- Stays calm and respectful while holding the line; escalates and documents.

**Red flags:** caves to "keep it open anyway" without a safety basis; offers no contingency thinking.

## Scenario 3 — The Asbestos Discovery, Live

**Interviewer (project lead):** "The contractor's just hit something that might be asbestos in the wall they're cutting. They've stopped. The job's already a day behind. What now?"

**Press-harder follow-ups:**
- "Can't they just tape it over and carry on at the other end?"
- "Testing will take days. We don't have days."
- "The contractor says they've seen this stuff before and it's fine."

**What good sounds like:**
- Stop, secure and vacate the area immediately — non-negotiable.
- Check the asbestos register / management survey.
- Competent assessment or sampling before anyone continues; no carrying on "at the other end".
- Rejects contractor reassurance as evidence; records the find and actions.

**Red flags:** lets the job continue nearby; accepts the contractor's word; prioritises the schedule over exposure.

## Scenario 4 — The Missing Records Audit

**Interviewer (auditor/board):** "I've asked for your water temperature records and there's a two-month gap. Walk me through how this happened and what it means."

**Press-harder follow-ups:**
- "So you can't actually prove the system was safe during that period?"
- "Whose fault is this?"
- "How do I know it won't happen again?"

**What good sounds like:**
- Owns the gap honestly without blame-shifting or minimising.
- Treats it as both a compliance gap and a real Legionella risk: reinstated monitoring, assessed the period, increased flushing, considered sampling.
- Explains the systemic fix (competent person, cover, review date) so it does not recur.
- Frames records as the defence and shows the recovery trail.

**Red flags:** blames the departed technician and stops there; downplays the risk; no preventive fix.

## Scenario 5 — The Direct Board Chair Message

**Interviewer (board chair, informal):** "Quick one — a visitor complained the restaurant was warm yesterday and now I'm hearing about a fire alarm being switched off. Is the site actually safe to keep open today? Straight answer."

**Press-harder follow-ups:**
- "I don't want a lecture, I want a yes or no."
- "Should I be worried about the headlines here?"
- "What are you doing about it right now?"

**What good sounds like:**
- Gives a clear, honest position — safe to trade, with the controls in place — or names what must happen first.
- Explains the alarm isolation was permit-controlled with fire-watch cover, time-boxed, areas informed.
- Translates into the chair's language: visitor experience, reputation, continuity.
- Calm, concise, no bluff; offers a short update plan rather than waffle.

**Red flags:** vague reassurance with no basis; technical jargon dump; bluffing that everything is fine without knowing.

## Scenario 6 — The Simultaneous Incidents

**Interviewer (operations):** "Right now: a chiller's down in the restaurant, a contractor needs the fire alarm isolated to work, and someone's just reported a flooded car park. You can't be in three places. Talk me through the next thirty minutes."

**Press-harder follow-ups:**
- "Which one do you drop if you have to?"
- "Who are you calling first and why?"
- "The board chair is also ringing you. Do you answer?"

**What good sounds like:**
- Triages by risk: life-safety controls first (alarm isolation only with fire watch; flooding as a slip/traffic hazard), then continuity (chiller contingency).
- Delegates clearly rather than trying to do everything personally.
- Keeps a simple decision log and one point of contact.
- Communicates priority order and reasoning calmly.

**Red flags:** treats all three as equal; no delegation; drops a safety item to answer the phone.
