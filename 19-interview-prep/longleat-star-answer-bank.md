## Purpose

A bank of worked **STAR** answers for the most common Longleat-style FM interview themes. Use them as models, not scripts. Adapt the structure to your own real experience and say them out loud until they sound like you.

STAR keeps a spoken answer tight and complete:

- **Situation** — set the FM context in one or two sentences.
- **Task** — the risk, duty or decision you owned.
- **Action** — what you actually did, step by step.
- **Result** — the outcome, the evidence, and what you learned.

> The interviewer is listening for: did you prioritise safety, did you make a decision, did you keep evidence, and can you frame it in business terms?

## The Five Core Stories to Prepare

If you prepare nothing else, prepare one real STAR story for each of these. Almost any question can be answered by adapting one of them.

1. A **safety call** you made (or would make) under pressure.
2. A **contractor** you challenged on competence, RAMS or behaviour.
3. A **budget or whole-life cost** decision you defended.
4. A **leadership or conflict** moment with your team.
5. An **incident** you managed from first report to close-out.

## Theme 1 — Visitor Safety Under Peak Footfall

**Question style:** "Tell me about a time you put safety ahead of operations."

- **Situation:** During peak trading, a public walkway became a slip hazard after a spill near a busy entrance.
- **Task:** Keep visitors safe without needlessly closing a key route during the busiest part of the day.
- **Action:** I made the area safe immediately — cordon and warning signs — diverted the flow to an alternative route, got it cleaned and dried, briefed the operations team, and logged the hazard and actions.
- **Result:** No injury, the route reopened within the hour, and the incident log supported a review of spill response. The principle I work to is make it safe first, then solve the inconvenience.

**Adapt for:** falling object near works, overcrowding, trip hazard on a path, weather-related hazards.

---

## Theme 2 — Animal and Site Infrastructure

**Question style:** "How would you manage FM risk around animal or specialist infrastructure?"

- **Situation:** A boundary fence on the edge of a public area was found damaged, with a possible containment and public-safety implication.
- **Task:** Control an estate risk where safety, containment and unclear responsibility all overlapped.
- **Action:** I made the area safe and restricted immediately rather than waiting to confirm whose responsibility the boundary was, escalated to the relevant specialist team, checked the deeds/agreement for ownership, and arranged the repair with the right competence.
- **Result:** The immediate risk was controlled the same day and responsibility resolved afterwards. The lesson: when safety or containment is in question, act first and settle ownership later, with everything recorded.

**Adapt for:** specialist plant serving animal facilities, water systems, power resilience to critical infrastructure.

---

## Theme 3 — Fire and Life-Safety Systems

**Question style:** "Tell me about a time you dealt with a fire-safety failure."

- **Situation:** A fire door on a public escape route was repeatedly wedged open for trolley access.
- **Task:** Restore compartmentation and the escape route without just losing the argument again next week.
- **Action:** I removed the wedge, explained to the team why a held-open fire door defeats smoke and fire control on an escape route, arranged an alarm-linked hold-open device so the door closes automatically on activation, and recorded it.
- **Result:** The route stayed protected and convenient, the behaviour stopped because the system removed the temptation, and a recurring audit finding was closed. I fix the cause, not just the symptom.

**Adapt for:** alarm fault during opening hours, contractor needing alarm isolation, blocked escape route, missed door inspection.

## Theme 4 — Water Hygiene Governance

**Question style:** "How do you make sure water hygiene does not slip?"

- **Situation:** I found a water temperature monitoring record with a two-month gap after a technician left.
- **Task:** Treat a compliance gap and a genuine Legionella risk, not just a paperwork hole.
- **Action:** I reinstated monitoring immediately, assigned a competent person, assessed the period of missing data, increased flushing on low-use outlets, arranged sampling where appropriate, and recorded the gap and the recovery actions.
- **Result:** Control was re-evidenced quickly and the gap was closed with a clear audit trail. The principle: a monitoring gap is a risk to manage and document, never something to quietly ignore.

**Adapt for:** reopening a closed wing, dead legs and low-use outlets, an L8 audit question.

---

## Theme 5 — Suspected Asbestos During Contractor Work

**Question style:** "A contractor uncovers a suspected asbestos material mid-task. What do you do?"

- **Situation:** During intrusive works, a contractor exposed a material that could contain asbestos.
- **Task:** Protect people and control the area without overreacting or ignoring it.
- **Action:** I stopped the work immediately, had the area vacated and secured, checked the asbestos register/management survey, arranged a competent assessment/sampling before anyone continued, and recorded the find and the actions.
- **Result:** No exposure, work resumed safely once cleared, and the asbestos record was updated. Stop, secure, verify, record — in that order.

**Adapt for:** any unexpected hazardous discovery, a RAMS that does not match the real task.

---

## Theme 6 — Power Outage and Business Continuity

**Question style:** "Tell me how you would handle a major power outage during open hours."

- **Situation:** A power outage hit during peak trading, affecting lighting, tills and some safety systems.
- **Task:** Keep people safe and the site running or safely paused, and communicate clearly.
- **Action:** I confirmed life-safety systems first (emergency lighting, fire detection, evacuation readiness), established what was on backup, prioritised visitor safety and clear communication, coordinated with operations on whether to pause or evacuate areas, and kept a decision log with times.
- **Result:** Visitors stayed safe and informed, critical areas were managed on priority, and the event fed a review of resilience and backup cover. Life safety first, then continuity, then communication.

**Adapt for:** plant failure in a heatwave, flood, system failure affecting a revenue area.

---

## Theme 7 — Maintenance Backlog and Reduced Budget

**Question style:** "You have a growing backlog and a cut budget. What do you do?"

- **Situation:** I inherited a growing maintenance backlog with a tighter budget and several ageing critical assets.
- **Task:** Reduce risk with limited money without cutting compliance.
- **Action:** I protected statutory and safety-critical spend, built visibility through an asset register and condition data, prioritised the backlog by risk and consequence, shifted spend toward planned maintenance, and presented the deferred risks so leadership accepted them knowingly.
- **Result:** The highest-risk items were addressed first, compliance stayed intact, and the backlog trend started to improve with evidence to show it. I spend limited money on the biggest risks, defensibly.

**Adapt for:** capital business case, planned vs reactive argument, choosing between two assets.

---

## Theme 8 — Board-Level Risk Communication

**Question style:** "How do you communicate FM risk to a board?"

- **Situation:** The board saw FM as an overhead and questioned the spend.
- **Task:** Reframe FM as a business enabler and protect the spend that cannot be cut.
- **Action:** I translated FM into their language — visitor experience, reputation, revenue continuity and cost — and showed concrete consequences: a failed chiller closes a restaurant, a poor path is a claim, a missed inspection becomes a headline. I brought evidence, whole-life cost and the cost of doing nothing.
- **Result:** The board understood FM as protecting revenue and brand, and the critical spend was protected. I never bluff a figure; if I do not have it, I commit to send it by a stated time.

**Adapt for:** "why this role", justifying investment, defending a budget line.

## Spoken-Answer Self-Check

After each practice answer, score yourself against the [marking guide](../17-marking-guides/marking-guide.md). A strong spoken answer:

- [ ] sets the situation in under 20 seconds;
- [ ] names the risk or duty clearly;
- [ ] makes a decision and explains why;
- [ ] prioritises life safety and compliance;
- [ ] frames the outcome in business terms;
- [ ] mentions the record and the follow-up;
- [ ] finishes — does not ramble.

## Two Opening Answers to Prepare

- **"Tell me about yourself"** — 60 seconds: who you are as an FM, the kind of estate/operation you run, one signature strength (e.g. safety-led decision-making), and why this role.
- **"Why this role at Longleat"** — 60 seconds: the operational FM challenge of a large visitor attraction (safety under footfall, mixed and specialist infrastructure, board-level stakes), and what you bring to it.
