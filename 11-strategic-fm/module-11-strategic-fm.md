## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this module, you should be able to:

- explain the difference between operational, tactical and strategic FM;
- align FM with the wider business objectives, not just the building;
- use data and KPIs to drive decisions rather than react to failures;
- plan over a multi-year horizon with lifecycle and risk in mind;
- decide what to keep in-house and what to outsource, and why.

## Phone Lesson 1: Operational, Tactical, Strategic

FM works on three levels at once.

- Operational is today: the leak, the call-out, the failed door, the safe running of the site this shift.
- Tactical is this year: the planned maintenance programme, the budget, contracts, audits and projects.
- Strategic is the next three to five years: where the estate is going, what it needs to support, and what investment that requires.

Most FMs are strong operationally and survive tactically. The step up to Level 4 thinking is being able to lift your head out of today's job and connect FM to where the organisation is heading. Strategic FM asks not "what is broken?" but "what will the business need from this estate in three years, and are we ready?"

## Phone Lesson 2: Align FM to the Business, Not the Building

FM exists to support the core purpose of the organisation. For a visitor attraction that purpose is a safe, memorable visitor experience that protects the brand and grows revenue.

That changes how you prioritise. A boiler is not just an asset; it is what keeps a restaurant open in peak season. A path is not just tarmac; it is the visitor route and a trip-claim risk. Strategic FM ties every decision back to the things the business actually cares about: safety, reputation, continuity, cost and growth.

When you propose work, frame it in business terms. Not "the AHU is end of life" but "this asset is the single point of failure for our busiest revenue space, and we have no resilience in peak season." That is the language that gets investment.

## Phone Lesson 3: Data, KPIs and Planning Ahead

Strategic FM is evidence-led. You cannot plan five years out on gut feel.

Useful FM measures include:

- statutory compliance percentage (the one that must be 100%);
- planned versus reactive maintenance ratio (more planned is healthier);
- asset condition and remaining life;
- response and rectification times against SLAs;
- energy use and cost trends;
- backlog of deferred work and the risk it carries.

The point of data is not the dashboard; it is the decision. A rising reactive ratio and a growing backlog tell you the estate is drifting toward failure and needs investment now, before something closes in peak season. Strategic FM uses that signal to plan, sequence and fund work before the crisis, not after.

## Practical FM Application

The board asks for a five-year FM plan for the estate. Reactive jobs are rising, the maintenance backlog is growing, and three major plant items are over fifteen years old.

The correct FM response is to build a lifecycle and risk-based plan: an asset register with condition and remaining life, a phased capital replacement programme sequenced by risk and revenue impact, a shift of spend from reactive to planned, clear KPIs to track progress, and each recommendation tied to business outcomes of safety, continuity, cost and visitor experience. The plan answers what the business will need and what it must invest to get there.

## Scenario Questions

### Question 1

The business wants to grow visitor numbers by 20%. What does that mean for FM and the estate?

### Question 2

You have a limited capital budget and three ageing assets that all need replacing. How do you decide the order?

### Question 3

Should grounds maintenance be kept in-house or outsourced? How do you decide?

## Hard Questions

### Hard Question 1

Explain how you would move an estate from reactive firefighting to planned, strategic FM.

### Hard Question 2

How do you make the case for FM investment to a board focused on visitor experience and revenue?

## Model Answers

### Model Answer 1

Growth increases load on every system: more visitors mean more wear, more waste, higher utility demand, busier toilets and catering, more parking and more pressure on safety and emergency capacity. I would assess whether current assets and infrastructure can take the extra load, identify the constraints that would fail first, and build the investment and resilience needed to support growth safely, rather than letting demand outrun the estate.

### Model Answer 2

I would prioritise by risk and business impact, not age alone. The asset whose failure would close a revenue area in peak season, breach compliance or endanger people goes first. I would consider condition, remaining life, consequence of failure, resilience (is there a backup?) and whole-life cost. I would phase the rest and document the deferred risk so the board accepts it knowingly.

### Model Answer 3

I would decide on cost, control, quality, risk and flexibility. In-house gives control, site knowledge and responsiveness; outsourcing gives specialist capacity, equipment and the ability to scale. I would compare true whole-life cost, the criticality of the work, how much specialist skill or kit it needs, and how tightly it must be controlled. Safety-critical and brand-critical work usually warrants tighter in-house control or a closely managed contract.

### Model Answer 4

I would start with stabilising compliance and safety, then build visibility: a complete asset register, condition data and a reactive-versus-planned baseline. I would shift spend gradually into planned maintenance, tackle the backlog by risk, and set KPIs to show the trend improving. Culturally I would move the team from "fix what breaks" to "prevent what we can," and report progress so the change is seen and funded.

### Model Answer 5

I would speak their language: visitor experience, reputation, revenue continuity and cost. I would show that FM protects the things they care about — that a failed chiller closes a restaurant, a poor path causes a claim, a missed inspection becomes a headline. I would present investment as protecting revenue and brand, with evidence, whole-life cost and the risk of doing nothing, so FM is seen as a business enabler, not an overhead.

## Marking Guide

Strong answers include:

- a clear operational/tactical/strategic distinction;
- FM aligned to business outcomes, not just buildings;
- risk- and data-led prioritisation;
- lifecycle and multi-year planning;
- evidence-based investment cases;
- a considered in-house versus outsource judgement.

Weak answers stay stuck in day-to-day operations, prioritise by age or noise, or justify FM as a cost centre rather than a business enabler.

## Interview Talking Points

Use this phrase:

> Strategic FM means lifting out of today's jobs and connecting the estate to where the business is going. I plan over a multi-year horizon, prioritise by risk and revenue impact, use data to act before failures, and present FM investment in the language of safety, reputation and continuity.
