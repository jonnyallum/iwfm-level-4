## Knowledge

**Q1.** Explain the difference between revenue (opex) and capital (capex) spend with an FM example of each.

**Q2.** What is whole-life cost and why is it better than lowest purchase price?

## Application

**Q3.** You are forecasting an £18,000 overspend in month seven. What do you do and why does timing matter?

**Q4.** Finance asks you to cut 10% from the FM budget. Where do you look first, and what do you refuse to cut?

## Judgement

**Q5.** A 12-year-old air handling unit has been repaired three times this year from the reactive budget. Build the case to move it to capital replacement.

**Q6.** The board believes reactive repair is cheaper than planned maintenance. How do you challenge that?

## Model Answer Points

- **Q1.** Opex is recurring running cost (reactive repairs, maintenance contracts, utilities, consumables). Capex is one-off investment in a lasting asset (new boiler, roof, chiller). Patching a failing asset from revenue year after year hides a capital decision.
- **Q2.** The total cost over an asset's life: purchase, energy, maintenance, downtime, failure risk and disposal. A cheap unit that fails, wastes energy and closes a revenue area costs more than a dearer reliable one.
- **Q3.** Flag it immediately to finance and your manager — no surprises. Separate one-offs from structural problems and bring a recovery plan (defer discretionary work, re-phase, or a justified ask). Early bad news is recoverable; month-eleven surprises are not.
- **Q4.** Protect statutory/safety-critical lines (fire, water, gas, electrical, lifting, asbestos). Look for savings in discretionary work, decoration, consumable waste and re-tendering. Model the risk behind any cut so the board chooses knowingly.
- **Q5.** Use evidence: three repairs' history and cost, food-safety and visitor-experience risk of failure in peak season, energy saving of a new unit, and five-year whole-life cost of repair vs replace. Present replacement as lower cost and lower risk with a payback.
- **Q6.** Reactive only looks cheaper because failures have not happened yet. Show the cost of one major unplanned failure in peak season versus the annual planned cost, plus the compliance risk of running safety-critical assets to failure.

## Marking Criteria

| Band | What it looks like |
|---|---|
| Distinction | Clear opex/capex thinking; protects statutory spend; argues on whole-life cost; runs a no-surprises forecast; builds evidenced capital cases. |
| Pass | Correct distinctions; sensible cuts and forecasting; reasonable business case. |
| Borderline | Knows definitions but defends spend in maintenance terms only. |
| Not yet | Treats budget as fixed; would cut compliance to hit a target. |
