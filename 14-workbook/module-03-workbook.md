## Knowledge

**Q1.** What does the Health and Safety at Work etc. Act 1974 require of an employer in broad terms?

**Q2.** What is the difference between "so far as is reasonably practicable" and "absolute" duties?

## Application

**Q3.** A near-miss is reported: a visitor nearly walked under a falling object near works. Nobody was hurt. Why does this still matter and what do you do?

**Q4.** What is RIDDOR and give two examples of reportable events on a visitor attraction.

## Judgement

**Q5.** A manager wants to keep an area open for trade, but you believe it is unsafe. How do you handle the disagreement?

**Q6.** An HSE inspector arrives unannounced. What do you want to be able to show them within ten minutes?

## Model Answer Points

- **Q1.** To ensure, so far as is reasonably practicable, the health, safety and welfare of employees and others affected, through safe systems, training, information, supervision and a safe environment.
- **Q2.** Reasonably practicable balances risk against the cost/effort of control; absolute duties must be met regardless of cost. Most general duties are SFAIRP; some specific ones are absolute.
- **Q3.** A near-miss is a free warning. Record it, investigate the cause, fix the control (segregation, exclusion zone), and learn before it becomes an injury. Outcome luck is not control.
- **Q4.** Reporting of Injuries, Diseases and Dangerous Occurrences Regulations. Examples: a specified injury to a visitor taken to hospital; a dangerous occurrence such as a lifting equipment failure.
- **Q5.** Safety is not negotiable; make the area safe or close it. Explain the risk and liability in business terms, offer a controlled alternative if possible, escalate and document rather than yield to commercial pressure.
- **Q6.** Risk assessments, statutory compliance records (fire, water, gas, electrical, lifting), training records, accident/near-miss log, permits and contractor RAMS — current and accessible.

## Marking Criteria

| Band | What it looks like |
|---|---|
| Distinction | Treats near-misses as prevention; refuses to trade safety for trade; can evidence compliance instantly. |
| Pass | Correct legal duties and RIDDOR; investigates near-misses; prioritises safety. |
| Borderline | Knows the Act but weak on application or evidence. |
| Not yet | Reactive only; would let commercial pressure override safety. |
