## Knowledge

**Q1.** What checks should happen before a contractor is approved to work on site?

**Q2.** What are RAMS and what makes them good rather than generic?

## Application

**Q3.** When is a permit to work needed, and give three examples of work that requires one.

**Q4.** What does proportionate contractor monitoring look like (not standing over every job)?

## Judgement

**Q5.** A long-standing contractor says they do not need induction "after all these years". How do you respond?

**Q6.** A contractor starts using a different chemical from the one in their RAMS. What do you do?

## Model Answer Points

- **Q1.** Competence and qualifications, insurance, safety policy and record, references, RAMS quality, relevant safeguarding/public-facing checks, and expiry tracking. Approval reviewed annually and after poor performance.
- **Q2.** Risk assessments and method statements. Good RAMS are task- and site-specific: actual task, location, sequence, hazards, controls, competence, equipment, emergencies and site interfaces. Generic RAMS list irrelevant risks and miss the real ones.
- **Q3.** When normal RAMS are not enough for high-risk work. Examples: hot works, roof/height access, electrical isolation, fire alarm isolation, confined space, lifting operations.
- **Q4.** Checking controls at a frequency matched to risk: confirming the permit, spot-checking high-risk controls, verifying close-out, and being willing to stop work if controls are missing.
- **Q5.** Familiarity is not evidence of current rules or hazards. Require induction or a proportionate refresh, keep it documented. Be polite but firm; the standard does not bend for reputation.
- **Q6.** Pause the task. Check the new product, its safety data sheet and COSHH assessment, and whether controls remain valid. Do not let work continue until the change is assessed and approved.

## Marking Criteria

| Band | What it looks like |
|---|---|
| Distinction | Treats contractors as part of the site risk profile; insists on site-specific RAMS, permits and evidence; will stop work; never bends to familiarity. |
| Pass | Knows approval, RAMS, permits and monitoring; manages changes sensibly. |
| Borderline | Knows the steps but relies partly on trust. |
| Not yet | Relies on contractor reputation instead of evidence and control. |
