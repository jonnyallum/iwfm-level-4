## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this module, you should be able to:

- explain the difference between revenue (opex) and capital (capex) spend;
- build and defend an FM budget line by line;
- explain whole-life cost instead of lowest price;
- manage variances, forecasts and accruals without surprises;
- justify FM spend to a board in business language, not maintenance language.

## Phone Lesson 1: Opex, Capex and Why FMs Confuse Them

Two pots of money run an estate.

Revenue spend (opex) is the day-to-day running cost: reactive repairs, planned maintenance contracts, consumables, utilities, cleaning, small parts. It repeats every year.

Capital spend (capex) is investment in an asset that lasts: a new boiler, a roof, a chiller, a building system replacement. It is a one-off that adds or extends value.

FMs get into trouble when they patch a failing asset out of revenue year after year because they cannot get capital approved. That hides the real cost and delays the decision that should have been made. A good FM names the problem clearly: this is no longer a repair, it is a replacement, and it belongs in capital.

## Phone Lesson 2: Build a Budget You Can Defend

A budget is a prediction you have to stand behind. Build it from evidence, not from last year plus a percentage.

For each line, know:

- what the cost is for;
- whether it is fixed (contract) or variable (reactive, utilities);
- what drives it (footfall, weather, asset age, statutory frequency);
- what last year actually cost, not what was budgeted;
- what risk sits behind it if you cut it.

Always protect statutory and safety-critical lines first: fire, water hygiene, gas, electrical testing, lifting equipment, asbestos management. These are not discretionary. You can flex decoration and projects; you cannot flex a Legionella regime or a fire door inspection.

## Phone Lesson 3: Variance, Forecast and No Surprises

A budget holder's real job is the forecast, not the budget. The budget is set once; the forecast is updated every month.

- Variance is the gap between what you planned to spend and what you did spend.
- A favourable variance is not automatically good. Underspend on planned maintenance often means work was not done.
- An adverse variance is not automatically bad. A failed chiller in a heatwave is a real cost, not poor control.

The cardinal rule is no surprises. Finance and the board can accept bad news early. They cannot accept a £40,000 overspend that appears in month eleven with no warning. Flag the pressure the month you see it, with a recovery plan or a clear ask.

## Practical FM Application

A 12-year-old air handling unit serving a visitor restaurant fails for the third time this year. Each repair is £3,000 from the reactive budget. A replacement is £28,000 of capital.

The correct FM response is to stop treating it as reactive. Build a short business case: three years of repair history, the food-safety and visitor-experience risk of another failure in peak season, the energy saving of a modern unit, and the whole-life cost of repair versus replace. Present replacement as the lower-risk, lower-cost decision over five years, and ask for capital with a clear payback.

## Scenario Questions

### Question 1

Finance asks you to cut 10% from your FM budget. Where do you look first, and what do you refuse to cut?

### Question 2

You are forecasting an £18,000 overspend in month seven. What do you do?

### Question 3

A contractor quote is the cheapest by far but the company is new and the spec looks thin. How do you evaluate it?

## Hard Questions

### Hard Question 1

Explain whole-life costing to a board member who only wants to know the cheapest price.

### Hard Question 2

Build the case for a planned maintenance budget when the board believes reactive repair is cheaper.

## Model Answers

### Model Answer 1

I would protect statutory and safety-critical lines first: fire, water hygiene, gas, electrical, lifting and asbestos. I would look for savings in discretionary areas such as decoration, non-urgent projects, consumable waste and contract re-tendering. I would model the risk behind any cut so the board chooses a saving with full knowledge of the consequence, rather than cutting blindly into compliance.

### Model Answer 2

I would flag it immediately to finance and my line manager rather than waiting. I would explain the cause, separate one-off events from a structural problem, and bring a recovery plan: deferring discretionary work, re-phasing projects, or a justified request for additional budget. The principle is no surprises and an early, evidenced ask.

### Model Answer 3

Cheapest is not the same as best value. I would check competence, insurance, references, the quality and specificity of the spec and RAMS, and whether the price is realistic for the scope. An unrealistically low price usually means missing scope, weak controls or change requests later. I would evaluate on whole-life cost and risk, not headline price.

### Model Answer 4

Whole-life cost is what an asset costs over its entire life: purchase, energy, maintenance, downtime, failure risk and disposal. A cheaper unit that fails twice a year, uses more energy and shuts a revenue area is more expensive than a dearer, reliable one. I would show the five-year total cost of each option, including the cost of failure during peak trading, so the decision is based on real cost, not just the invoice on day one.

### Model Answer 5

Planned maintenance is cheaper than reactive over time because it prevents failures, extends asset life, reduces emergency call-out premiums and protects revenue areas from unplanned closure. Reactive looks cheaper only because the failures have not happened yet. I would show the cost of a single major unplanned failure in peak season against the annual planned cost, and the compliance risk of running safety-critical assets to failure.

## Marking Guide

Strong answers include:

- a clear opex versus capex distinction;
- protection of statutory and safety-critical spend;
- whole-life cost rather than lowest price;
- early variance flagging and a no-surprises culture;
- evidence-based business cases for capital;
- FM spend justified in business and risk language.

Weak answers treat the budget as a fixed number, cut compliance to hit a target, or defend spend only in maintenance terms a board cannot act on.

## Interview Talking Points

Use this phrase:

> I manage FM money as risk and value, not just cost. I protect statutory spend, I make decisions on whole-life cost rather than lowest price, and I run a no-surprises forecast so finance hears about pressure early, with a plan attached.
