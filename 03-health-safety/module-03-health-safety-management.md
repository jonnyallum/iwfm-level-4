# Module 3: Health & Safety Management

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this module, you should be able to:

- explain the Facilities Manager's role in health and safety management;
- connect legal duties to day-to-day FM controls;
- use risk assessment, evidence and escalation as management tools;
- identify when contractors, defects or workplace conditions create unacceptable risk;
- describe how to prove health and safety compliance during an audit or interview.

## Phone Lesson 1: The FM Health and Safety Role

Facilities Managers do not own every health and safety risk in an organisation, but they often control the building, contractors, maintenance activity and physical workplace.

That means FM decisions affect fire doors, slips and trips, plant rooms, access routes, equipment, asbestos, water systems, electrical safety and contractor work.

Good FM health and safety management is built on four questions:

1. What can harm people?
2. Who could be harmed?
3. What controls are in place?
4. What evidence proves those controls work?

## Phone Lesson 2: From Law to Control

The Health and Safety at Work etc. Act sets the broad duty to protect employees and others. The Management Regulations turn that duty into risk assessment, planning, competent advice, monitoring and review.

For FM, this means a defect is not just a maintenance issue. It may be a legal control failure.

Example:

- A loose stair nosing is a workplace safety issue.
- A contractor drilling into unknown fabric is an asbestos risk.
- A blocked electrical cupboard is an access and fire risk.
- A missing risk assessment means the decision trail is weak.

The FM response should be practical: make safe, assess, assign ownership, record evidence and review completion.

## Phone Lesson 3: Evidence and Escalation

A control that is not checked can quietly fail.

Facilities Managers need evidence that inspections, maintenance, training, contractor checks and corrective actions are happening. Evidence does not need to be complicated, but it must be clear, current and retrievable.

Escalation matters when a risk cannot be fixed immediately. A good escalation record shows:

- what the risk is;
- what temporary controls are in place;
- who owns the action;
- when it is due;
- who has been informed;
- whether the risk is still tolerable.

## Practical FM Application

A weekly workplace inspection finds damaged stair edging, stored items in a plant room and an out-of-date contractor RAMS pack.

A weak response is to send three emails and hope people act.

A strong response is to log each issue, apply immediate controls, assign owners, set due dates, photograph evidence, escalate high-risk items and review the pattern at the next health and safety meeting.

## Scenario Questions

### Question 1

A department asks FM to reopen an area before a leak-damaged ceiling tile has been inspected. How do you decide whether it is safe?

### Question 2

A contractor arrives to drill into a wall, but the asbestos register is unavailable and the job is urgent. What do you do?

### Question 3

A recurring defect is fixed three times but keeps coming back. How should you manage it as a health and safety issue?

## Hard Questions

### Hard Question 1

Explain how you would build a monthly FM health and safety dashboard that a senior manager could use to make decisions.

### Hard Question 2

A senior stakeholder says your risk controls are slowing down operations. Explain how you would respond without becoming defensive or careless.

## Model Answers

### Model Answer 1

I would keep the area closed until the ceiling has been checked by a competent person. The risk could include falling material, hidden water damage, electrical contact or mould. I would record the defect, isolate the area, arrange inspection, agree remedial work and only reopen when the competent advice and evidence show it is safe.

### Model Answer 2

I would stop the intrusive work. Urgency does not remove the asbestos duty. I would check whether the building could contain asbestos, locate the register or survey, and obtain competent advice if the information is missing or unclear. If the work cannot be proven safe, it must not proceed.

### Model Answer 3

Repeated recurrence suggests the root cause has not been controlled. I would move it from a simple defect to a risk-managed action, investigate the underlying cause, review whether the temporary controls are adequate, escalate cost or design decisions if needed and track the permanent solution to completion.

### Model Answer 4

The dashboard should show leading and lagging indicators: overdue statutory tasks, high-risk defects, incident trends, inspection completion, contractor RAMS compliance, training gaps, audit actions and repeated issues. It should show owners, due dates and risk levels so managers can remove blockers and prioritise resources.

### Model Answer 5

I would explain the purpose of the control, the risk it manages and the legal or operational consequence of bypassing it. I would look for a practical way to reduce delay, such as better planning or earlier permit review, but I would not remove a control unless the risk remains properly managed.

## Marking Guide

Strong answers include:

- clear identification of risk;
- immediate action to make safe;
- competent advice where needed;
- ownership and escalation;
- evidence of decisions;
- review of repeat failures;
- proportionate language rather than panic or complacency.

Weak answers rely on informal judgement, ignore legal duties or treat evidence as optional paperwork.

## Interview Talking Points

Use this phrase:

> I manage health and safety through practical controls: risk assessment, competent input, evidence, escalation and review. My aim is to keep the operation running, but not by normalising unsafe conditions.
