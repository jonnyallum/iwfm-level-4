# Module 7: Asset Management

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this module, you should be able to:

- explain what an FM asset register is and why it matters;
- connect assets to risk, compliance, cost and lifecycle planning;
- distinguish maintenance, inspection, replacement and improvement;
- prioritise backlog using criticality and evidence;
- answer interview questions about asset data and maintenance planning.

## Phone Lesson 1: Assets Are More Than Equipment Lists

An asset is something the organisation owns or controls that needs management. In FM, this includes plant, equipment, building fabric, systems, statutory items and sometimes infrastructure.

Examples:

- boilers;
- lifts;
- distribution boards;
- fire doors;
- emergency lights;
- water outlets;
- roofs;
- gates and barriers;
- access equipment;
- animal enclosure infrastructure in a visitor attraction.

An asset register is useful only if it supports decisions. It should show location, condition, criticality, maintenance needs, compliance duties and ownership.

## Phone Lesson 2: Planned Maintenance and Lifecycle

Planned preventive maintenance is designed to reduce failure. Lifecycle planning looks further ahead and asks when an asset will need major repair, refurbishment or replacement.

Reactive work fixes a problem after it appears. Planned work tries to prevent the problem. Lifecycle work prevents the organisation being surprised by expensive failure.

Good FM balances all three:

- keep critical assets safe now;
- maintain them properly;
- plan replacement before failure becomes an emergency.

## Phone Lesson 3: Backlog, Budget and Risk

Most FM teams have more work than budget. Asset management gives a defensible way to prioritise.

A strong prioritisation method considers:

- life safety;
- statutory compliance;
- public or operational impact;
- asset condition;
- failure history;
- repair cost versus replacement cost;
- lead time;
- reputational risk.

The FM must be able to explain why one job is funded before another.

## Practical FM Application

You have one budget pot and three issues: faded decoration in a public cafe, an ageing lift with repeated entrapments and roof leaks affecting a plant room.

Asset management helps you prioritise. The lift affects safety, accessibility and visitor confidence. The roof leak may threaten electrical or mechanical assets. Decoration matters, but probably sits lower unless it creates safety or brand risk.

## Scenario Questions

### Question 1

An asset register lists 300 assets but many have no condition rating. What is your next step?

### Question 2

A boiler is serviced annually but repeatedly fails in winter. What does that tell you?

### Question 3

A senior manager wants to remove planned maintenance to save money. How do you respond?

## Hard Questions

### Hard Question 1

Design a simple asset criticality scoring system for an FM team.

### Hard Question 2

Explain how you would turn a maintenance backlog into a board-level decision paper.

## Model Answers

### Model Answer 1

I would not rely on an incomplete register for decisions. I would prioritise condition surveys for critical assets first, update missing data, assign owners and use contractor reports, service history and site inspections to improve the register.

### Model Answer 2

Annual servicing alone is not controlling the risk. I would review fault history, age, parts availability, maintenance quality, operating conditions and whether replacement or upgrade is more sensible than repeated reactive repair.

### Model Answer 3

I would explain the risk of false savings. Removing planned maintenance can increase breakdowns, compliance failures, emergency call-outs and asset replacement costs. I would offer risk-based optimisation, but not blind removal of critical tasks.

### Model Answer 4

A simple score could rate life safety, statutory duty, business impact, public impact, condition, failure history and replacement lead time from 1 to 5. High scores get priority for maintenance, monitoring and capital planning.

### Model Answer 5

I would group backlog by risk and consequence, identify legal and life-safety items, show cost bands, explain deferred risk, propose quick wins, list decisions needed and give options such as fund now, phase, monitor or accept risk formally.

## Marking Guide

Strong answers include:

- asset register discipline;
- condition and criticality;
- planned versus reactive thinking;
- lifecycle awareness;
- defensible budget prioritisation;
- evidence from service history and defects.

Weak answers treat asset management as a spreadsheet exercise or prioritise based only on complaints.

## Interview Talking Points

Use this phrase:

> I use asset data to make better decisions: what we own, how critical it is, what condition it is in, what evidence we have and what risk we carry if we defer work.
